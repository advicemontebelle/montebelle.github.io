Neuroscience has shown how a powerful narrative can stimulate the emotions, builds engagement and long-term memory encoding of the message.

There are three benefits to the power of storytelling that particularly chime with marketing: comprehension, motivation and memory. There is plenty of evidence to illustrate the fundamental power of story to influence our thoughts, feelings, learning, memories and behaviour, but its true power is in its ability to influence implicitly, without our conscious knowledge.

There has been a recent surge in the number of major advertisers claiming to base their marketing strategy around the concept of ‘transmedia storytelling’. Jonathan Mildenhall, Coca-Cola's global strategy chief, cites Coke's ‘happiness factory’ campaign as offering something “much more brand-centric and designed to build ‘brand mythology’, whereas basic branded content... invariably involved a trade-off between consumers and advertisers and failed to harness long-lasting support from its audiences”. He believes that effective transmedia storytelling should include a combination of “an immersive world, heroes, villains, friends and foes, a compelling story and multiple access points”.

It is ironic, because for a few years, there had been a definite emphasis on the multiple access points and rather less on the storytelling element of great advertising. The fact that multinationals the size of Coca-Cola, Ford, Procter & Gamble and Unilever now employ storytelling specialists and base much of their marketing spend around their craft, can perhaps be linked back to an Indian proverb: “If you tell me a fact, I will learn. If you tell me a truth, I will believe. But tell me a story and it will live in my heart forever.”
How to Influence Through Storytelling

Storytelling

It is those last two words - ‘heart’ and ‘forever’ - that provide the key to the scientific reason why storytelling is such a potent form of communication. They refer to the emotions and long-term memory emanating from the two parts of the brain that are most active, relative to other media experiences, when people are exposed to narrative-led audio-visual entertainment, particularly cinema and TV.

PHD's Neuroplanning study (Admap, January 2010) showed that it was the amygdala, responsible for our emotional processing, and the hypothalamus, which governs our long-term memory encoding, which were most active when watching film/TV content, compared with newspapers, posters or magazines. If we pause to consider what that means, even without the enhancing effects of context, it was narrative-led AV content that set the two parts of the brain flowing that represent where brands live; in our emotions and in our long-term memories.
