This article describes some of the ways in which digital technology is changing branding, emphasising the importance of brand-building as a way to secure future sales.

Savvy, better informed consumers require brands to move away from 'push' mass media advertising and towards 'pull' marketing that focusses on quality, value and 'brand-budding'. Emotional pull is important, but easily undermined by bad reviews and customer scepticism. Product-led brand-building which use functionality, experience and sensory innovation will be important, and the article describes examples of each. As connectivity and experience continue to increase in importance 'brand-budding' - where brands partner or connect with each other - may improve success. I like this question. It rightly assumes that brands will remain important in future, and doesn't invite any silliness about them being a quaint marketing concept that can't survive the rise of technology. The question also raises the current industry debate up from 'communication engagement' to 'brand-building'. The moment we start believing that 'engagement' is our end goal, then we are heading for, what my son would describe as, #epicfail.

I think it is worth making a commercial point about brands before we dive into this question. There should be no tension between the notion of 'brand' and the notion of 'sales'. I mention it because the two are often seen as somehow in opposition with each other â€“ 'You can either build brand equity, or, achieve sales'. In fact, the only difference between 'sales' and 'brand' is time; brands are a promise of future sales. Let's briefly examine how brands have been built in the past and interrogate that model's relevance for the future.

Brands were considered to be rational product offerings augmented with intangible, emotional values. The right blending of those two things created an impression, or personality, in the minds of consumers and influenced their preference. The 'blind-branded' test was the ultimate arbiter of this added emotional value. The emotional values of a brand were largely imbued through the power of long-term association, usually in the dominant and fantasy world of TV advertising. Put a cowboy next to some cigarettes for long enough... put a puppy next to a loo roll for long enough...

This theory of brand-building has served us well for decades, but several things are beginning to weaken its continued relevance (at least in certain parts of the world). The ever-more sophisticated consumer has developed a somewhat sceptical eye towards marketing. Emotional added value can become seen as an unconvincing veneer. Increasingly, people are wary of brand-building efforts as 'spin' or 'jazz hands'. People are not suddenly rejecting marketing, or disliking brands but, in the main, they are pretty fluent now about the difference between the 'sizzle' and the 'sausage', and they won't be sold short on the pork.

The democracy of information has allowed consumers to focus hard on the more rational and 'real' aspects of the product itself. Social commentary and ratings have particularly brought tangible product performance itself to the fore. The most emotionally rich brands can be undermined by a one-star product rating. This is as true for products as it is for the companies that make them. Corporate misbehaviour plays out publicly these days and influences people's perception of the brands those companies make. Increasingly, consumers are able to access product and company truths that lie behind the branded perceptions of them.

TMuch has been said about the decline of 'push' marketing tools, like broadcast advertising, that were the perfect stage for playing out the emotional association game of brand-building. In part, brand-building theory was a function of brand-building practice â€“ because brands were built using 'association' in mass media, brand-building theory was forged around notions of intangible, emotional value. But as mass media fades, that theory fades with it.
