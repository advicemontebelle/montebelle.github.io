This article examines five areas that offer new sources of growth and how businesses can assess the opportunities they might present in a structured, systematic way.

They are: new markets, which are in practice more complex than is generally believed; shifting social values and behaviours that create new market spaces; money shifting between different consumer groups; emerging technologies, which are unpredictable and often slow; new business models, which must be carefully assessed whether they are fit for market.

Finding and developing new markets is one of the core activities of business. Looking for growth is more challenging in a post-crisis world where consumers are more considered in their decisions, and resource pressures push up costs for everybody.

This article, which summarises a longer Future Perspectives report, Unlocking New Sources of Growth, focuses on where new sources of growth can be found, and how businesses can identify and assess them in a structured way. The growth framework in this report is based on extensive research into how markets change and where opportunities emerge.
What Are The New Sources of Growth?

Growth sources

Finding growth is about looking for new pools of money. At risk of oversimplifying, these tend to come from one of four places: demographics; changing values; economics; and technological change. The full report has more detail on each of these.

People – mostly in emerging markets – who have reasonable amounts of disposable income for the first time, for example the 'emerging middle classes'. Conventional wisdom is that these represent easy wins because of the recent rates of growth seen in these markets. In practice these opportunities are more complex – and perhaps less immediate – than is generally believed. One example of new market change is the way European companies are taking advantage of the wave of Chinese tourists who are discovering travel outside China (see the panel 'China's tourism boom: European response', page 30).
