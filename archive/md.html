This briefing offers an overview of the history, theories and key trends related to decision making by consumers. It describes the development of approaches such as the AIDA model to other theories premised on multiple systems of thinking and peer-to-peer influence. The report outlines evidence for different decision making approaches in the healthcare and financial services markets.

Definition: Decision making is the cognitive process of selecting a course of action from among alternatives. In communications circles, the study of decision making has facilitated the development of analytical models of how to influence consumer behaviour.

Quick facts

  • 70% of consumer decisions are made at the point of purchase
  • Use of the internet speeds up consumers' decision making ability
  • Recent research has found that the type of flooring (carpet versus vinyl tiles) affects how consumers perceive products and therefore their decision making .

The study of consumer decision making can be traced back at least as far as 1738, when Daniel Bernoulli developed the first formal explanation of the process. It was later extended by von Neumann and Morgenstern, and called the Utility Theory.

Making Decisions

This theory proposed that rational consumers make decisions based on the expected outcomes of their decisions; Nobel Laureate Herbert Simon proposed an alternative, simpler 'Satisficing' model in the mid-1950s. He argued that consumers got to approximately where they wanted to go and then stopped the decision-making process, i.e. they did not need to analyse all the information available

In the late 1970s, two psychologists, Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky, developed the Prospect Theory, which added two major elements to the Satificing model; the concepts of value (replacing the utility found in Utility Theory) and endowment, in which an item is more precious if one owns it than if someone else owns it. This thinking led to the development of a series of decision-making strategies that marketers adopted depending on the product segment.

In parallel in 1898, the American advertising and sales pioneer, E. St. Elmo Lewis developed the AIDA funnel model to explain the mechanisms of consumer decision making. This hierarchical, rational four-layer process used the four cognitive phases that buyers follow when accepting a new idea or purchasing a new product; Awareness, Information, Desire and Action. In 1911, Arthur Sheldon extended the model with a fifth phase, 'permanent satisfaction' to stress the importance of repeat sales.

In the following decades, the AIDA model served to show how advertising affects consumers and was the basis for numerous motivational-driven consumer behaviour research models.

By 2000 marketers were realising that "funnel" models were too simplistic. Neurosciences has provided completely new insights into how the human brain works, and this has called into question many of our assumptions about how people respond to advertisements; how they reply to market research questions; how they actually take decisions. In particular, this work has focused attention on the dominance of emotional responses in much of what we do as consumers, and on the degree to which much of what we do is done below the level of (self)consciousness .

Earls hzx suggested that because humans are social animals, much of our behaviour and responses can best be interpreted on a collective, herd basis. Much of what we do is without conscious thought: we simply pick up and act on clues from our environment, in effect subconsciously. These patterns of behaviour become, in time, habits.

Gordon contends that a great deal of our brand consumption is effectively habitual, and few purchasing decisions involve anything that a decision theorist would recognise as a decision process. With most consumers, behaviour is unconscious, non-rational and influenced by primitive emotions. We absorb, react and 'intend' without knowing why or even being aware of the true drivers. Subsequently we use language (thought) to become conscious of it to ourselves or to explain it to others. She concluded that the AIDA model has been transformed into the concept of 'a customer journey'. A person moves through distinct passive and active phases or sequences depending on category and brand. E.g. from the emerging need, to active consideration, researching, short-listing, purchase and finally post-purchase.

This takes a diverse range of reference points that are all distilled by consumers prior to them making a decision. While more complex, the eventual outcome is a better, more solid relationship between consumers and brands.

In recent years the internet has brought a change in the way consumers can make decisions; they are privy to far more information about the product from the company, reviews and from a plethora of "influentials". The means by which we market goods and services, and the opportunities to market new products, have changed profoundly. Word of mouth is now far more important than it was in the days of pure mass media ix . Consumers are now empowered to make more informed decisions and demand more of a two-way dialogue on how goods and services develop.
