The 'expected utility theory' deals with the analysis of choices among risky projects with (possibly multidimensional) outcomes. Bernoulli argued that the paradox could be resolved if decision makers displayed risk aversion and argued for a logarithmic cardinal utility function. The first important use of the expected utility theory was that of von Neumann et al, who used the assumption of expected utility maximization in formulation of game theory.

Game theory attempts to mathematically capture behavior in strategic situations, in which an individual's success in making choices depends on the choices of others. Today, game theory is a sort of umbrella or 'unified field' theory for the rational side of social science, where 'social' is interpreted broadly, to include human as well as non-human players (computers, animals, plants).

The funnel approach to decision-making suggests that before purchasing, the consumer will have an emerging need that will cause them to start considering the brand or category. According to this theory, they will have a 'long-list' of brands in mind and will do research of some kind to whittle down their options to a shortlist before choosing a product

The AIDA model describes the basic process by which people become motivated to act on a purchase. The motivation to make a purchase depends on:

  • AWARENESS of the existence of a product or service
  • INTEREST in paying attention to the product's benefits;
  • DESIRE for the product.
  • ACTION, the natural result of moving through the first three stages

This basic AIDA model, which is still used extensively today, has been built on to include many more variables, in particular a fifth stage, Experience. Other models include AIUAPR – Awareness, Interest, Understanding, Attitudes, Purchase and finally Repeat Purchase.

Key Theories on Making Decisions

These models however are now widely seen as being too rational and structured. Emotion is now known to play a large role in consumers' purchase decisions, and work in the area of heuristics (Gordon 2002) shows that we often bypass this structured approach to decisions. Also work by Styler at Brann Worldwide (Styler 2001) introduced the concept of advice-led buyers, those relying heavily on what other people say and less on their own research.

Psychologists Stanovich and West coined the term 'two-system thinking' in 2000. It proposes the presence of two minds in a single brain; however the two systems have an entirely different way of working. It is how these two systems work and interact with each other that determine the decisions we make. System 1 is intuitive, emotional, unconscious, fast, automatic, effortless and associative (interprets stimuli); System 2 is reasoning, logical, conscious, controlled, slower, needs effort, and is rule-governed. System 1 is more primitive, and thought to have evolved to protect from danger; unlike System 2, it is instantly decisive. System 1 differs from System 2 in the following ways: status quo bias (it prefers what it knows), greater sensitivity to loss than gain, response to immediate rather than deferred expectations. System 1 is thus a major influence in why people form habits and resist change. System 1 influences System 2 in almost all decisions; the emotional prompt avoids the hard work of rational decision, which is relatively rare.

In recent years ethnography xi has enabled us to look at consumer decision making in more depth, and studies have found that the decision process has a number of stages (creating a decision context, building a decision framework, finessing that framework and finalising the decision. However, it's not neat and tidy – these stages can happen in tandem, omitted or looped. It is not related to length of time it takes, and differs from one individual to the next and for first time versus repeat purchase); Emotion plays a key role in decision making.
